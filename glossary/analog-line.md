# Analog Line

## Definition

An analog line is a traditional telephone line that transmits voice and data using continuous electrical signals, as opposed to digital signals. Analog lines are the backbone of the legacy Public Switched Telephone Network (PSTN), supporting devices like telephones, fax machines, and modems. While once ubiquitous, analog lines are gradually being replaced by digital and VoIP technologies.

## How It Works

Analog lines carry signals as varying voltages, faithfully reproducing the sound of a voice or the tones of a fax. These lines connect homes and businesses to the telephone network via copper wires. Analog lines are simple, reliable, and compatible with a wide range of devices, but they have limitations in speed, quality, and scalability compared to digital alternatives.

## Real-World Application

For decades, analog lines powered voice communication, emergency services, and business operations. They remain in use for legacy systems, alarm lines, and rural areas where digital infrastructure is lacking. However, as networks modernize, analog lines are being phased out in favor of more advanced solutions.
