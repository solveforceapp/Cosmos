# Absorption Spectroscopy

Absorption spectroscopy is an analytical technique used to measure the concentration and properties of molecules by observing how they absorb light at specific wavelengths. In biotechnology and neurotechnology, this method is essential for identifying biomolecules, monitoring chemical reactions, and studying cellular processes.

## Principle and Mechanism

When light passes through a sample, certain wavelengths are absorbed by the molecules present, causing electronic, vibrational, or rotational transitions. The resulting absorption spectrum acts as a molecular fingerprint, revealing the identity and quantity of substances in the sample.

## Applications in Biotechnology

- **DNA and Protein Analysis:** Absorption spectroscopy is used to quantify nucleic acids and proteins by measuring absorbance at characteristic wavelengths (e.g., 260 nm for DNA, 280 nm for proteins).
- **Enzyme Kinetics:** Researchers monitor changes in absorbance to study enzyme-catalyzed reactions in real time.
- **Drug Development:** The technique helps screen and characterize pharmaceutical compounds by their absorption profiles.

## Applications in Neurotechnology

- **Brain Imaging:** Near-infrared absorption spectroscopy (NIRS) is used for non-invasive monitoring of brain oxygenation and blood flow.
- **Neurochemical Sensing:** Absorption-based sensors detect neurotransmitters and other brain metabolites.

Absorption spectroscopy is a cornerstone of modern bioscience, enabling precise, rapid, and non-destructive analysis of complex biological systems. Its versatility makes it indispensable for research, diagnostics, and innovation in biotechnology and neurotechnology.
